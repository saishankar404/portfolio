We define stages of sleep by what your **EEG** does; that's the thing with all the wires hooked up to your head that looks at what big clumps of neurons are doing.

You _drift off_ into:

**Stage 1:** sleep, which is light and can have some consciousness, such that people will report being awake if you nudge them (**_I was just resting my eyes._**)

You then go into deeper stages of sleep,

**Stage 2:** no dreams, easy to wake up (**_an afternoon 10-minute nap_**)

then,

**Stage 3:** deepest sleep, most important and refreshing, can dream a little but boring/ordinary.

Stages **1–3** are called **non-REM_.**

Then **REM** happens, then you either wake briefly or go back into another cycle of **1–2–3-REM**.  
These cycles _last_ about **1.5–2 hours** and happen through the night, with more **stage 3** _sleep early_ and more **_REM sleep_** later.

**REM** is one of the single weirdest things that a **_brain_** _does_.

Going from deep sleep, the brain switches **EEG** patterns to looking more like you're awake, but all your muscles shut off including and all your neurons that use

- **serotonin** (_maintain your mood_)
- **nor-epinephrine** (_keeps you alerted & focused_)
- **histamine** (_helps you staying awake_)

stop **firing** completely, with a big shift towards **acetylcholine-using neurons**(neurons that help you **_think, move, remember, and dream_**_._ kind of a big deal.)

and your **motor neurons** (_responsible for movement of muscles_) are temporarily stopped by a mechanism in the brainstem, which causes **muscle-atonia**(_paralysis_)

Your eyes start to move **inconsistently** (_hence the name, Rapid Eye Movement=REM_) and your **heart rate**, **temperature**, **breathing**, and **blood** **pressure** get **_irregular_**. Your vivid and weird dreams, happen in this stage.

The human brain is a fascinating place.  
It is composed of approximately **_80 billion neurons_** and it is their **combined** **electrical chatter** that generates oscillations known as **brain waves**.

There are five types of **brain waves** — _alpha_, _beta_, _theta_, _delta_, and _gamma_ that each indicate a different state between sleep and wakefulness.

![Brain Waves](/assets/blog/image-2.png)

- **delta waves :** the slowest waves, linked to deep, dreamless sleep and physical healing.
- **theta waves :** occur during light sleep and deep relaxation, often tied to dreams and creativity.
- **alpha waves :** produced when we daydream, meditate, or listen to the sound of rain.
- **beta waves :** linked to active thinking, focus, problem-solving, and everyday alertness.
- **gamma waves** : the fastest waves, connected to high-level cognitive functions like learning and memory.

Your brain _randomly_ fires signals at the **pons**, which is a part near your brain-stem. This firing causes you to experience visual information from the dream world, even though your eyes are closed.

But, during dreaming, bits and pieces of these memories seem to be **reorganized** to create a particularly **bizarre scenario**:  
_you're talking to your friend when it suddenly begins to rain flowers and out of nowhere you're dancing at some random party._

## sleep walk and hypnic jerks

You don't dream right away during a sleep cycle.  
Right before your dreaming period begins, your body releases chemicals that have a paralyzing effect.

In a sleep walker, the dreaming period outlasts the paralyzing effect.  
This is usually caused by high **melatonin** (_a chemical that helps regulate sleep_) levels.

Most people's **serotonin** levels decrease and changes in **_neurotransmitter_** _levels_ as they _approach_ _puberty_ which is why most **_sleepwalkers_** are **children**.  
High **stress** can **_trigger_** your body to produce more **_serotonin_** because your body thinks you need more **deep,** **restful** **sleep**.

Sleep walking usually occurs in **stage 3** of the **Non-REM** sleep where the person brain _partially_ wakes up from deep sleep but the person remains in a **_state between sleep and wakefulness_**.

the **motor control** areas of the brain are **_active_** (_allowing movement_), but the **_consciousness_** and **_reasoning areas_** (_prefontal cortex_) are still mostly asleep.

Normally, your brain transitions smoothly through sleep stages.  
In sleepwalkers, this is **_interrupted_**, so the person is **mobile** but **not aware**. (_as person is_ **_not_** **_conscious_**, they typically **_won't remember_** _anything_)

Scientifically its not possible to dream simply because dreams happen in REM stage and slepwalking happens in NREM(non-REM) stage.  
but… the **_exceptions_** exists.

Ever felt like you're **_dropping_** from a height while asleep?  
You suddenly **panic**, even though you're just lying there — then your body gives a quick **jolt**, and everything feels normal again. it's called a **hypnic jerk**, and it happens to a lot of us.

![Hypnic Jerk](/assets/blog/image-7.png)

It's **normal** and usually don't **disrupt** sleep.

Dreams of falling may trigger the _sensation_ of a **hypnic jerk**, as if your body is **_reacting_** to the **dream**. It's like your mind is _visualizing_ the fall, and your body **jolts** to simulate trying to catch yourself. Even though you're still in the early stages of sleep.

**_Hypnic Jerks_**, are still **not fully understood** by science  
They may be are a leftover reflex from our primate ancestors — when they relaxed in trees, the brain would misinterpret muscle relaxation as falling and jolt the body awake to prevent a fall.
